OrderYOYO
Brief introduction to the company

OrderYOYO is a Danish startup focusing on helping takeaway restaurants transition into the digital space. OrderYOYO provides restaurants with their own branded web and mobile platform, administration system for tracking orders, monthly revenue etc. In addition to this, the company provides further services such as marketing material, increasing customer engagement and technical support. OrderYOYO has been operating since 2013, grown to over 60 staff members, expanded by opening a second office in London along with entering new markets by offering its services in United Kingdom, Ireland and Germany.

The problem
Unmaintained code base and low conversion numbers

OrderYOYO provides website template that is branded personally for each restaurant that signs up for the service. In the website, users are able to learn more information about the restaurant, acquire contact information and open a seperate ordering system to access restaurants menu, users account information and place an order.

Two main problems with previous website templates where the fact that conversion numbers were low and from back-end perspective the website and ordering system were developed in two different coding languages. Based on user testing and gathered feedback, both the website and the ordering system were considered visually outdated and “bland”.

Gathering data
Demographic analysis and user surveys

During initial wireframe testing and throughout prototype testing we simultaneously gathered data about our current and potential users. Their habits, motivations to order food and overall demographic. To better understand for whom we are creating the website and what would be the best approach. Based on the data, we were able to create accurate personas that would help us design a website for them.

Solution
Overhaul interface and improve ordering flow

Due to the amount of problems the old system was causing, we needed to completely revamp the code base. But because of short window of time for the project, we decided to keep the overall system similar to the old one as to not spend more time on developing completely different ordering, payment flow and overall websites structure. We decided improve current UI/UX to focus on achieving the core KR (Key Result) for this project - the conversion of visitors into “first order customers” (zero to one order). We aimed to create more modern look and feel for the product to entice people into exploring the website and restaurants menu. After which the improved ordering process would help the user in making a successful order easier. As seen in picture B1 we did, however, go through ideation process to map out future features and implementations, and to decide exactly how we could easily improve the old system.

Cleaning up
Merging two projects into one

It was decided to merge two previous projects (website template and ordering system) into one, fully functional website with same code-base and interface design. It would allow for more seemingless ordering experience on the website because we would eliminate the ordering pop-up and also improve Google Search results for restaurants, since previous ordering system containing restaurants menu could not be crawled by search engines.

Google’s Material Design
Delivering familiar and trustworthy interface

Reasons for introducing Material Design into our project varies from bringing more familiar interface to the user, to keeping consistent design to maintain the feeling of uninterrupted ordering flow. Utilizing the guidelines of a proven and rich design system also allows us to develop design system of our own in the future and manage easier front-end development by creating components. This decision also sped up interface design process after wireframes and mockups were tested.

Ordering menu
More categories less items

During user research where we A/B split tested collapsed and expanded menu type, we quickly approved that users prefer to firstly see food categories and only then the actual items. That was the driving factor in our decision to show collapsed menu categories on initial view. This decision also contributed to lessen the confusion and initial “bombardment” of menu items.

Checkout
Helping user finish the order

In efforts to make ordering flow faster, we decided to cut down on page load times and combine necessary information into single checkout page. During user testing of checkout screen, results showed that information displayed on the screen was understandable on desktop and mobile devices. And users managed to finish the order.

After soft launch of the website, we gathered data about user conversion and noticed drops in checkout screen. This was caused by people getting confused about information displayed, and some people not noticing payment information section nor the button to finish the order. We addressed the issue by “minifying” user details and order information on mobile devices. We also moved payment information on top of the screen, to allow users to quickly finish the order in checkout screen if there are no issues with user or order details.

Conclusion
Ongoing agile development

Our aim is to provide fast and simple ordering experience tailored to user needs, which is why we are maintaining data-driven design approach. I am focusing on analysing data from PowerBI, Google Data Studio, Analytics and HotJar to identify and come up with solutions for pain points during the ordering process and use insights from our audience to tailor the user experience. By combining previous website and ordering system into one website template, we have seen 50% of recorded sessions scroll through the menu. Besides improving that number, our focus is to push users to create profile and make an order.
